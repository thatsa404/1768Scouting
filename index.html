Team Performance Metrics ▼

This app pulls data from three different sources, each using a different method to estimate how good a team is. Understanding how each number is calculated helps you decide how much to trust it — and when to trust your own eyes instead.

STATBOTICS — Expected Points Added (EPA)

EPA is a statistical model that estimates how many points a team contributes to their alliance's score, on average, per match. It uses every match that team has ever played — not just at this event — so it arrives pre-loaded with a track record before a single match is played here.

How it works: After each match, the model looks at the alliance's total score and adjusts each team's EPA up or down depending on whether the alliance scored more or less than expected. Teams that consistently help their alliances outscore predictions earn a higher EPA; teams that drag scores down earn a lower one. It's similar to how sports analytics models (like baseball's WAR) credit or penalize individual players based on team outcomes.

What the "ceiling" is: The ceiling is a projection of where EPA could realistically reach by the end of the season, fitted to each team's improvement curve. It's most useful early in the season.

Strengths
  • Works before the event starts
  • Accounts for schedule strength
  • Breaks into auto / teleop / endgame
  • Huge historical dataset behind it
Limitations
  • Doesn't know how points were scored
  • Can be slow to reflect sudden improvements
  • Relies on official score data, which can have errors
THE BLUE ALLIANCE — Offensive Power Rating (OPR)

OPR is a simpler, purely math-based estimate of how many points a team contributes to their alliance per match, calculated only from matches played at this event.

How it works: Each match gives us an equation: Team A + Team B + Team C = Alliance Score. Once enough matches are played, we have a large set of these equations — one per alliance per match. OPR solves them all simultaneously using a technique called least-squares regression, finding the individual scores that best explain every alliance total. It's the same kind of math used to fit a line through a scatter plot.

Why it needs several matches: With only a few matches played, there aren't enough equations to solve reliably, so OPR is unstable early in the event. By qualification round 20+, it usually settles into a stable and trustworthy estimate.

Strengths
  • Based entirely on real scores at this event
  • Not biased by prior-season performance
  • Easy to verify — the math is transparent
Limitations
  • Unreliable until ~6–8 matches are played
  • Only knows total alliance score, not who did what
  • Teams that always play together can be confused by the model
SCOUTING — Custom Game Model (2026 REBUILT)

Scouting data comes from human observers watching each match and recording exactly what each robot does — which shift they played in, whether they scored fuel, whether they climbed, and how effective their scoring was. This app processes those observations into a custom EPA estimate built specifically for the 2026 game.

How the scouting EPA is calculated: For each team and each match, scouts record actions (Scorer / Shuttler / Defender / Died per shift, fuel counts, climb level). These are averaged across all observed matches to get rates — for example, "this team scores fuel in 80% of their active shifts." A scoring model then converts those rates into an expected point contribution, similar to EPA.

What TBA fusion does: Raw scouting estimates can be noisy — scouts aren't always sure exactly how many fuel pieces each robot scored. When TBA match breakdown data is available, this app uses a technique called share-weighting: it takes the real total fuel count from the official breakdown, then divides it among the three alliance partners proportionally to their scouted contribution rates. The result is more accurate than either source alone.

The shift percentages shown use conditional probabilities: Because the 2026 game alternates which hub is active each shift, a team can't score fuel in a shift where their hub is off. Rather than penalizing a team for a hub that was inactive, the app only counts matches where that hub was active when computing the shift scorer percentage. This gives you a true "how often did they score when they could" rate.

Strengths
  • Only source that knows how points were scored
  • Works immediately — one match is enough
  • Captures things the score doesn't: defending, dying, skill
  • Fused with official data when available for extra accuracy
Limitations
  • Quality depends entirely on scout accuracy and attention
  • Data entry errors can skew results significantly
  • Fewer matches observed = less reliable averages

Which should you trust most? The short answer: use all three together. Before the event, EPA gives you the best head start. Once 15+ matches are played, OPR becomes a strong sanity check. Scouting data is your best window into what a team actually does on the field — especially for alliance selection, where understanding roles matters more than raw score totals. When all three agree, you can be confident. When they disagree, that's a signal to watch that team more carefully.

Watch List — Match Outcome Simulation ▶

The Watch List simulates the remainder of the qualification schedule to estimate win probabilities, ranking point probabilities, and projected final standings. The process runs in six stages, each building on the last.

Stage 1 — Alliance Score Prediction

For each unplayed match, each team is assigned a predicted scoring contribution. If a team has individually ignored matches, their adjusted OPR (recomputed without those matches) is used directly. Otherwise, OPR and EPA are blended based on how many matches have been played:

w = min(1, matchesPlayed / 30)
contribution = w × OPR + (1 − w) × EPA

At match 0 the prediction is 100% EPA — which is pre-calibrated from the full season and reliable before a single match is played. By match 30 it is 100% OPR, which by then has enough data to outperform the season prior. Between those extremes the two sources are weighted proportionally. If only one source is available, it is used alone; if neither is available, the event average OPR is used as a last resort.

The same blend is applied when computing residuals, so the residual distribution reflects errors relative to whatever prediction the model would have made at the time of each match.

Stage 2 — Score Uncertainty Model

Score predictions are never exact. The model uses relative residuals — the percentage by which an alliance's actual score differed from prediction — to characterise how uncertain each prediction is. Unlike absolute residuals (where a +80pt error on a 500pt alliance gets applied to an 80pt alliance), relative residuals scale correctly: the same 20% overperformance means +100pts for a strong alliance and +16pts for a weak one.

From each played match: relResidual = (actualScore − predicted) / predicted

Early-event prior: When few matches have been played, the empirical residual pool is small and unreliable. The model supplements it with a Gaussian prior derived from Statbotics EPA standard deviations. Statbotics reports the match-to-match scoring variability (σ) for each team — exactly what we need. Assuming team contributions are statistically independent, the alliance-level uncertainty is:

σalliance = √(σ₁² + σ₂² + σ₃²)

This is converted to a relative scale (σ_rel = σ_alliance / predicted) and used to generate 10 synthetic Gaussian samples that are mixed into the empirical pool. At 0 matches played the prior dominates; by ~15 matches the empirical data (~30 residuals) provides 75% of the weight and the prior fades naturally.

The blended pool is built per alliance, since each set of three teams has a different σ. If no EPA standard deviation is available, a flat 18% coefficient of variation is used as a fallback.

Stage 3 — Win & RP Probability (Monte Carlo)

Each unplayed match is simulated N=500 times (fewer for lopsided matches — see Stage 6). In each trial, both alliance scores are sampled proportionally from their blended residual pools:

simScore = max(0, predicted × (1 + sample(blendedPool)))

Win probability is the fraction of trials where one alliance outscored the other. A difference of less than 1 point is counted as a tie.

Threshold-based RPs (e.g. Energized RP at 100 fuel, Supercharged at 360): A fuel-specific OPR is solved from played match breakdowns, giving each team a predicted fuel contribution. The alliance fuel total is then sampled with the same proportional noise, and the RP is awarded if the simulated total clears the threshold.

Binary RPs (e.g. Traversal RP): the historical achievement rate across all played matches for that set of teams is used directly, without sampling.

Stage 4 — Standings Simulation

With per-match probabilities in hand, the entire remaining schedule is simulated 500 times. Each simulation starts from teams' actual current ranking points, then samples win/loss and bonus RPs for every unplayed match using the Stage 3 probabilities. Teams are ranked by total RP at the end of each simulation.

Across 500 simulations, each team accumulates a distribution of final ranks. The app reports the mean rank (expected finishing position), the 10th-percentile rank (best realistic outcome), and the 90th-percentile rank (worst realistic outcome).

Stage 5 — Impact Analysis

For each unplayed match, the model asks: how much does the outcome of this match change the focused team's projected rank? Two forced-outcome simulations are run — one where red wins, one where blue wins — and the focused team's mean rank is recorded under each scenario.

impact = max(|baseline_rank − rank_if_red_wins|, |baseline_rank − rank_if_blue_wins|)

The number of simulation trials per match scales with the match's uncertainty: a 50/50 match gets the full 500 trials; a 99/1 match gets as few as 25, since its outcome is nearly predetermined and high precision isn't needed for a small impact.

Stage 6 — Match Prioritisation

The "Matches to Watch" list ranks external matches (those not involving the focused team) by a combined score:

priority = impact × uncertainty
uncertainty = 1 − |P(red wins) − P(blue wins)|

A match where Team A has a 95% win probability affects the focused team's rank in only 5% of scenarios — it's high-impact but low-uncertainty, so it ranks below a 60/40 match with similar stakes. This surfaces matches worth actually watching, not just matches whose outcome is already decided. The top 10 are shown; the focused team's own matches are always listed separately regardless of impact score.
